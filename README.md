# The Philadelphia AI Infrastructure Corridor

### A Strategic Proposal for Scalable, Urban-Integrated Compute Development

**Prepared by**  
The Morningstar Alliance  

**Author**  
Helel ben Shachar  

**Date**  
April 2026

  
Executive Summary 
The rapid acceleration of artificial intelligence has created unprecedented demand for compute infrastructure, energy, and talent. While existing hubs such as Northern Virginia and Silicon Valley continue to expand, capacity constraints, rising costs, and geographic concentration present growing challenges. 
Philadelphia represents a uniquely underutilized opportunity to establish a next-generation AI infrastructure corridor. 
With access to reliable energy (including nuclear), extensive fiber connectivity, major redevelopment zones, and proximity to world-class academic institutions, Philadelphia is positioned to support both hyperscale data center deployment and urban-integrated compute ecosystems. 
This proposal outlines a framework for developing Philadelphia as a Tier 1 AI infrastructure hub capable of supporting national and transatlantic workloads while catalyzing regional economic growth. 
  
1. Strategic Advantages of Philadelphia 
1.1 Energy Availability and Grid Stability 
Pennsylvania offers a diversified and resilient energy portfolio, including nuclear generation, natural gas, and established transmission infrastructure. This provides a critical advantage in supporting high-density compute environments where power availability is the primary constraint. 
  
1.2 Geographic Positioning 
Philadelphia sits within the Northeast Corridor between New York City and Washington, D.C., with access to major fiber routes and proximity to transatlantic cable landing infrastructure. 
This positioning enables: 
•	Low-latency connectivity across major population centers  
•	Efficient integration into existing internet backbone systems  
•	Strategic support for international data flows  
  
1.3 Land Availability and Redevelopment Opportunities 
The Navy Yard 
A controlled, infrastructure-ready environment with strong potential for secure, highdensity deployment. 
Refinery Redevelopment Site 
The former Philadelphia Energy Solutions refinery site represents one of the largest contiguous redevelopment zones on the East Coast. Its industrial legacy, scale, and existing infrastructure make it an ideal candidate for hyperscale data center campuses. 
  
1.4 Talent Pipeline and Research Ecosystem 
Philadelphia hosts a concentration of leading academic institutions, including: 
•	University of Pennsylvania  
•	Drexel University  
•	Temple University  
•	Villanova University 
 
 
 
These institutions provide: 
•	AI and engineering talent pipelines  
•	Research partnerships  
•	Workforce development capacity  
  
1.5 Workforce Retention and Livability 
Philadelphia offers a cost-of-living advantage relative to nearby cities while maintaining cultural depth, transit accessibility, and strong community identity. 
This supports long-term workforce retention—an increasingly critical factor in infrastructure planning. 
  
1.6 Strategic Autonomy and Economic Centrality 
In many established infrastructure hubs, particularly within the Washington, D.C. metropolitan region, technology development operates alongside and often subordinate to federal priorities. 
Philadelphia offers a distinct alternative. 
As a major city without a dominant federal presence, Philadelphia provides an environment in which technology infrastructure can emerge as a primary economic driver rather than a secondary or adjacent sector. 
This distinction has meaningful implications: 
•	Greater alignment between public policy and private infrastructure investment  
•	Reduced competition for political and regulatory attention  
•	Increased opportunity for anchor institutions to shape regional development.  
In this context, AI infrastructure development in Philadelphia would not compete for priority—it would define it. 
  
 
 
1.7 Regional Alignment and Federal Representation 
The Greater Philadelphia region sits within a uniquely dense corridor of federal representation, spanning Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Delaware. 
This tri-state area includes: 
•	multiple U.S. Senate delegations  
•	a significant concentration of House representation  
•	proximity to the Washington, D.C. policy ecosystem  
In addition, Philadelphia’s location within the broader Northeast Corridor places it adjacent to major economic and technological centers, including New York City and the Washington metropolitan region. 
This creates a layered advantage: 
•	Opportunities for coordinated, multi-state support of infrastructure initiatives  
•	Increased visibility within federal policy discussions  
•	Alignment with existing technology and economic priorities across the region  
As AI infrastructure becomes increasingly strategic at the national level, regions with strong federal connectivity and multi-state alignment will be positioned to accelerate development and secure long-term investment. 
  
1.8 Long-Term Energy Scalability and Advanced Generation Potential 
Sustained growth in AI infrastructure will require not only immediate power availability but long-term scalability. 
Pennsylvania’s existing nuclear energy footprint provides a meaningful strategic advantage in this regard. The state has: 
•	Established nuclear generation capacity  
•	Regulatory experience with nuclear operations  
•	Workforce familiarity with advanced energy systems  
While new nuclear development remains complex and highly regulated, regions with existing infrastructure and institutional experience are better positioned to evaluate and potentially support future expansion, including next-generation reactor technologies. 
This creates an opportunity for Philadelphia to align long-term AI infrastructure growth with advanced, stable, and low-carbon energy solutions. 
  
2. Development Model 
A multi-tiered approach is recommended: 
2.1 Hyperscale Campus Development 
•	Primary deployment at the refinery redevelopment site  
•	Designed for large-scale compute and future expansion  
2.2 Secure Infrastructure Zone 
•	Navy Yard as a high-security, controlled-access compute campus  
2.3 Urban Edge Compute Nodes 
•	Distributed, smaller-scale facilities integrated into the urban environment  
•	Supporting latency-sensitive applications  
  
3. Infrastructure and Transit Integration 
Expansion of public transit—particularly extension of the Broad Street Line—would significantly enhance workforce access to both the Navy Yard and redevelopment zones. 
Transit integration aligns with: 
•	workforce mobility  
•	sustainable urban development  
•	long-term infrastructure planning  
  
 4. Why Now 
•	AI compute demand is accelerating rapidly  
•	Existing hubs are approaching capacity limits  
•	Strategic diversification of infrastructure is becoming essential  
•	Philadelphia presents a rare convergence of:  
o 	energy  o 	land  o 	talent  o 	location  
  
5. Call to Action 
The Morningstar Alliance recommends: 
1.	Initiation of feasibility studies for large-scale deployment  
2.	Exploration of public-private partnerships  
3.	Identification of anchor tenants for early-stage investment  
4.	Engagement with local and state stakeholders  
  
6. Philadelphia: Culture, Community, and Workforce 
Philadelphia is more than its reputation for passionate sports fans. Day to day, it is a city where people are direct, grounded, and welcoming—where neighbors look out for one another and new arrivals quickly find their place. 
It is not called the City of Brotherly Love and Sisterly Affection by accident. That identity is reflected in daily life: in the way communities form, in the openness toward newcomers, and in the pride residents take in their city. 
 
 
 
Philadelphia offers a rare combination of strengths: 
•	a rich and diverse food culture  
•	world-class cultural institutions  
•	an established public transit system  
•	a dense network of leading universities  
As a sanctuary city, Philadelphia has demonstrated its commitment to inclusion and opportunity. That same spirit would extend to a new generation of workers and innovators. 
An emerging AI infrastructure sector would not be an isolated industry here—it would be integrated into the life of the city, supported by a population that values both progress and community. 
  
7. Closing Statement 
Philadelphia is not simply an available location—it is a strategic opportunity. 
It offers the infrastructure, workforce, and civic alignment necessary to support the next generation of AI development. With coordinated investment and vision, it can become a cornerstone of the emerging global compute landscape.
